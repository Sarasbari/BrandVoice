Software sprawl: A common challenge for knowledge workers

Today’s businesses face a critical challenge: the average company uses more than eighty-eight tools just to get day-to-day work done. The challenge of “software sprawl” struck a chord with many of our customer speakers and attendees. At Dojo, scaling from hundreds to over one thousand employees across multiple locations triggered an explosion in the number of workplace apps. Adnan Khan, co-CEO and co-founder of Viva, shared a familiar frustration: productivity loss from constantly switching between different tools. Meri Williams, CTO of Pleo, observed that people are overwhelmed—not just by the number of tools, but by the variety of functions and knowledge required to use them.

These aren’t isolated cases. Our recent partnership with Harvard Business Review (HBR) on a piece of joint research revealed how software sprawl hinders a company’s ability to manage organizational knowledge: while ninety-seven percent of organizations recognize the importance of managing organizational knowledge, only forty-four percent believe they’re doing it effectively. Even more telling, while eighty-six percent agree AI can improve knowledge management, only three percent of organizations currently use it.

Several of our customers who are pioneering the use of AI for knowledge management shared valuable insights at the events. Their recommendations include maintaining accurate, reliable sources of truth, appointing knowledge champions at your organization, and embracing AI for smarter information retrieval (“stop searching, start asking AI”).

Next-generation workers: The future is bright

One of the most inspiring aspects of the Showcases was witnessing the remarkable growth of our Campus Leaders and the expanding young-professional segment of our regional user base. This year alone, we welcomed over one hundred new Campus Leaders across France, Germany, and the UK—many of whom joined us at the events.

These young leaders are not only building thriving communities at their universities, but are also pioneering innovative ways of using Notion that are influencing how the next generation approaches work and collaboration. After all, by 2030, Gen Z and Millennials will make up nearly sixty percent of the workforce, so it’s exciting to see how they’re shaping the future how we work.
