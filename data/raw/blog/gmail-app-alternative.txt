If you ask the average person for their email address, chances are you’ll hear “@gmail.com.” That’s not surprising since over 30% of the world’s population uses Gmail. But while Gmail works well for basic use, it doesn’t always offer the tools needed for a truly productive workflow; when email becomes a core part of your workday, Gmail’s basic features can start to feel limiting.

Email isn’t just for personal communications anymore; it has become a big part of how we work, and sometimes, Gmail’s default features are incapable of handling this intertwining. When this happens, that’s your cue to start looking for a Gmail app alternative—something with more power and less clutter.

While plenty of modern apps offer smarter automation and deeper customization, finding the right one can be tricky. In this article, we’ll show you how to choose the best alternative to the Gmail app and highlight five top picks to boost your productivity.

A quick look at Gmail’s default features

Gmail is packed with features that have made it a go-to choice for email. It handles the basics well, allowing you to send, receive, and organize emails. You can set up labels to categorize your messages and use filters to sort incoming mail, which can help keep your inbox tidy to some extent.

Beyond the basics, it also offers some helpful extras, such as an advanced search bar, scheduled send features, and Smart Reply and Smart Compose to speed up email writing.

Gmail covers the basics well, but for users who want to unlock greater efficiency with advanced features, it can be limiting. As a result, many users are switching to smarter Gmail app alternatives designed to streamline workflows and boost productivity.

2. Outlook

For those looking for Gmail-like apps that feel familiar but offer more powerful features, Microsoft’s Outlook is often the first choice. It’s a long-standing email option that works well if you’re already using other Microsoft tools. Being a part of the Microsoft 365 core apps, the app focuses on delivering a range of functions that help you boost your email productivity.

Outlook gives you the basics to keep your email organized and manage your inbox, including:

Tools like folders, categories, and rules to help you sort and filter emailsA built-in calendar and task manager, allowing you to schedule appointments and keep track of your tasks directly from your Outlook app

Tools like folders, categories, and rules to help you sort and filter emails

A built-in calendar and task manager, allowing you to schedule appointments and keep track of your tasks directly from your Outlook app

While it prioritizes functionality, Outlook’s interface can feel cluttered and overwhelming, especially for new users. It doesn’t look as familiar or visually appealing as more modern email apps, so the layout can make navigation challenging and difficult to master.

Outlook offers customization options, such as color palettes and fonts, but if you need more robust customization features to set up your inbox view for specific workflows, the app can be a bit limiting.

Outlook does have some basic AI-assisted features to speed up your work. Focused Inbox helps auto-prioritize crucial emails, and the Suggested Replies feature helps you generate auto-responses to your emails. Sadly, some users have complained that this reply feature is not quite reliable.

If you’re looking for more advanced AI features, such as help drafting full emails, summarizing long emails, or auto-organizing your inbox, you may need to check out Microsoft 365 Copilot AI, which comes at an additional cost.

3. Fastmail

Fastmail is known for its reliability and focus on speed. It’s mainly suited for small teams but also serves as a solid alternative for users who prioritize performance and control. Fastmail has standard core features for organization and inbox management.

It gives you:

A clean and responsive interface that does a good job for email correspondence and reducing clutterExcellent support for folders, labels, and filtering rules, much like Gmail

A clean and responsive interface that does a good job for email correspondence and reducing clutter

Excellent support for folders, labels, and filtering rules, much like Gmail

These features give you some control over how your inbox is structured and how incoming mail is managed.

The app includes built-in calendar and contacts features, but that’s about it in terms of an integrated productivity suite. You might find that calendar functions lack the depth and intuitiveness of more established email platforms. While there are decent customization options—such as switching layouts and themes—don’t expect highly personalized inbox views tailored to your specific needs.

Fastmail primarily focuses on providing reliable email service—as of this writing, it doesn’t offer AI-powered features. Unlike most newer clients, it doesn’t have built-in AI tools for drafting emails or summarizing conversations, so you’ll need to do most of the work manually.

Good cross-platform support is available with Fastmail through web browsers and mobile apps (iOS and Android).

4. HEY Email

HEY Email’s core focus is reducing inbox anxiety through unconventional email actions. It offers a feature called The Screener as part of its key productivity features, forcing you to approve senders before they enter your “Imbox.” This helps reduce unwanted messages. The messages that do come in are sorted into categories like Impersonal or Paper Trail for items like receipts, so you can focus on specific types of emails at a time.

HEY’s interface is designed to reduce distractions and offers features like Reply Later and Set Aside that help you deal with emails when it’s most convenient. However, users have found the reply process to be more complicated than expected, with some claiming that replying to long threads can become chaotic, hindering the app’s intended simplicity. Customization in HEY is limited because the app is intentionally built to make you try emailing in a way you’re not used to. You can only put up cover art to block emails you’ve read.

Another disadvantage, compared to most modern clients, is that HEY doesn’t offer built-in AI-powered features. The design focuses on human intelligence over artificial intelligence, which may seem innovative, but is really just a nice way to make you spend more time managing your inbox.

Although HEY is primarily web-based, it also offers apps for mobile devices. It has a built-in HEY Calendar, but it largely functions as a standalone email service with no other integrations with broader ecosystems.

5. Spike

Spike aims to modernize the email experience by presenting messages in a conversational, chat-like format, simplifying communication and collaboration and making them more natural. It organizes your inbox into conversations, such as text messages, making email threads easier to follow.

Similar to Gmail’s Priority Inbox, essential emails are grouped together, while less critical messages are filtered into a Low Priority inbox. If you prefer traditional organizational methods, you should keep in mind that Spike doesn’t offer classic tags or folders for categorization.

The platform features a sleek, modern interface, making it look like a messaging app. This conversational layout is one of its standout features, making it especially user-friendly. Some of the key features include:

Online presence and read indicators that let you know when a recipient is online and has read your emailSpike Notes, which bring collaborative note-taking tools to your inbox, letting you and the other person co-edit and brainstorm within the email

Online presence and read indicators that let you know when a recipient is online and has read your email

Spike Notes, which bring collaborative note-taking tools to your inbox, letting you and the other person co-edit and brainstorm within the email

Although Spike’s chat-like design is intuitive, you may need some time to adjust to the nontraditional email setup. For customization, Spike allows you to switch email themes and set up notifications to suit your needs. As far as smart automation goes, Spike only offers Magic Message, an AI assistant that helps you draft emails and replies, brainstorm, and take notes.

The service is available on web, desktop, and mobile devices, and its seamless Cloud integration lets you attach files from Google Drive, Dropbox, and OneDrive. Spike is not a standalone email service—you’ll need to connect it to your existing email accounts.
