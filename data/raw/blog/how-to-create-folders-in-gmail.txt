Gmail folders vs. labels: What’s the difference?

If you’re new to Gmail or haven’t used it in a while, you might notice that this popular provider handles email organization differently than most other services. You might be wondering what the folders are called in Gmail and if they are the same as labels.

The short answer is that folders are not the same as labels in Gmail, so you won’t find a dedicated folders feature. Instead of traditional folders, Gmail uses a unique labeling system that serves a similar purpose to traditional folders but with important differences.

Labels act like tags that you attach to your emails, allowing you to apply multiple labels to a single message. These tags are visible both on the emails themselves and in Gmail’s left sidebar, where clicking on a label displays all emails associated with it. Keep in mind that labels are different from Gmail’s built-in Categories, such as Promotions, as well as System labels like Drafts, Trash, etc.

Unlike Gmail, most other email platforms use traditional folder systems, similar to physical filing cabinets. When you move an email to a folder, it’s physically relocated from your inbox and stored exclusively in that location. As a result, an email can only exist in one folder at a time, and to access it later, you’ll need to navigate directly to that specific folder.

How to make a folder in Gmail using labels

If you know your way around Gmail, you can use a label as a folder, even though this is not its default function.

All you need to do is create a label and move emails into it. This will remove the emails from other locations in your inbox. The emails in your label (turned folder) will only appear under that specific label and nowhere else, like a traditional folder.

Creating folders in Gmail using labels: Is it worth it?

While Gmail labels offer a flexible alternative to traditional folders, they can quickly become tedious and difficult to manage, especially as your inbox grows. Setting up a label system that actually helps often requires time and dedication. If you want to automate the process, you need to create detailed filters and assign rules for different senders or topics.

After creating specific auto-label rules for every sender or subject variation, you’ll need to constantly make manual adjustments to keep your inbox truly organized. This inbox micromanagement can quickly become a significant time and energy drain. And without the constant upkeep, these label-based “folders” often end up just as cluttered and overwhelming as an unorganized inbox.
