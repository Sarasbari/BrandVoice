Flexibility and ease of use

When you first open Microsoft 365 and Zoho Workplace, you’ll notice that the latter has a more streamlined feel due to excellent integration between different tools. Microsoft 365 feels like a bundled collection of platforms, while Zoho gives off the impression of a centralized workspace. It’s reminiscent of collaboration platforms like Slack, except it has more extensive features of a full-fledged productivity suite.

If convenience and fluidity are your priorities, Zoho Workplace might be a better option. This isn’t to say that Microsoft 365 is overly complex, but it does involve a steeper learning curve. Microsoft’s focus has always been on feature variety, so it’s a better option for advanced users.

Both Zoho Workplace and Microsoft 365 offer excellent cross-platform support, with desktop apps for Windows and macOS, web apps, and mobile apps. This adds more flexibility to your workflow.

Emailing experience

Microsoft 365 offers Outlook as the native email client, while Zoho Workplace comes with Zoho Mail. Both platforms are available independently, in which case you can use them for free. The main difference is that you can upgrade Zoho Mail as a standalone platform, while Outlook’s paid version is only a part of Microsoft 365 plans.

Outlook and Zoho mail are practically identical when it comes to the user interface. They feature a similar design with connected apps on the far left, email categories next to them, mail management features above the inbox, and split-square email viewing.

Overall, the design of both apps feels quite cluttered, with Zoho Mail having a slightly cleaner look due to fewer accent colors. There are far cleaner and more eye-pleasing options out there, but this isn’t a major issue unless you prioritize aesthetics.

Zoho Mail and Outlook are equipped with all the necessary email management features you’d expect from your email provider, such as:

A notable advantage of Zoho’s solution is the attachment limit, up to 1 GB, compared to Outlook’s 150 MB with a similarly priced plan. Another standout feature is the attachment viewer, which lets you preview the attachment without having to download it.

For example, Zoho Writer features Zia, an AI writing assistant that can perform several useful functions, most notably:

With Microsoft Word, you’d need third-party tools like Grammarly to unlock such functionalities, so having them built into the document editor is quite an advantage.

The main downside of Zoho’s editing apps is that they work in a closed environment. While you can download a Word or Excel doc and send it via email to anyone, there’s no such option with Zoho’s apps—unless you connect a Document API. You can only share documents through links, and the recipient must have a Zoho account to edit or comment on them.

With this in mind, internal collaboration might be smoother with Zoho Workplace, but file sharing outside your workspace is more challenging.

Productivity enhancement

Zoho Workplace and Microsoft 365 are both feature-rich productivity suites packed with tools that can help you boost your workflow. Zoho Mail’s Zia AI integration, for instance, streamlines email composition and offers quick summarization. The tool also features email scheduling, customizable filters, integrated tools within your inbox, and workflow rules to help you automate your emailing.

Microsoft 365 offers Copilot AI for advanced email assistance and deep task integration with Microsoft To Do. It seamlessly integrates with the Outlook calendar and various Microsoft applications. Microsoft 365 also offers powerful automation through Power Automate, an advanced way to streamline repetitive tasks across the full suite of tools, including Outlook Mail.

One advantage of productivity-first workspaces over traditional productivity email providers is that they give you full control over your work environment. This allows you to customize your workflow according to the way you work, the tools you use, and the projects you work on. It also enables you to seamlessly connect your email with other work apps for maximum efficiency.

As robust as these platforms seem, there’s a catch. Their advanced productivity features are only accessible through paid subscriptions, so you’ll need to upgrade to higher-tier plans to fully enjoy their capabilities.
